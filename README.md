# Otter Hammerhead Drysuit Restoration

A full hands-on restoration of a 10–15 year old Otter Hammerhead trilaminate drysuit, purchased secondhand. The suit had deteriorated latex wrist and neck seals, a failed zipper, and stitched seams that were allowing water ingress. I researched, sourced parts, fabricated my own tools, and completed every repair myself.

The suit has since been dived — it came up completely dry.

---

## Why This Project

Drysuit repair — especially zipper replacement — is a highly specialised skill. In the Victoria region of South Australia, there is effectively one person who replaces drysuit zippers: Stewart Donn, based in Mt Gambier. Most people simply post their suit to him. When I looked into doing it myself, more than a few people told me I wouldn't be able to pull it off.

I did.

## Materials & Tools

- **Adhesive:** SC4000 contact cement with hardener, ratio-mixed by weight on a kitchen scale
- **Seam tape:** Aquatape over contact cement on all stitched seams
- **Seal stock:** Sourced from [Seaskin](https://www.seaskin.co.uk/) after researching supplier options across Australia and the UK
- **Surface prep:** Acetone wipe-down and light sanding of all bonding surfaces
- **Roller:** Bought specifically at Bunnings to press seals and get good adhesion without air pockets
- **Respirator:** Borrowed a full respirator — SC4000 fumes are no joke. I'd heard of a repair technician who retired early due to health effects from years of working with similar adhesives. PPE was non-negotiable.

**Fabricated tools:**
- **Wrist expander:** Built from PVC pipe to hold the wrist opening round and taut while the latex bonded — keeping even pressure across the full circumference
- **Neck seal jig:** Cardboard former with double-sided tape to hold the neck seal in a circle and apply it as one smooth motion rather than in sections

## The Process

### 1. Seal Replacement

The old wrist seals had deteriorated and were no longer sealing. I removed them, cleaned the cuff area with acetone and sanded it back, then fitted new latex seals from Seaskin.

The PVC wrist expander tool was key — without it, keeping the seal round and evenly tensioned while the cement cured would have been guesswork. The neck seal was done using a cardboard ring with double-sided tape to pre-form the seal and press it down uniformly in one go.

All surfaces were prepped with acetone and light sanding before any adhesive was applied. SC4000 was mixed with hardener by weight.

### 2. Zipper Replacement

This was the hardest part of the project. The original Tizip zipper had failed. I sourced a replacement — but the new zipper was too long, and the previous zipper (done by someone else before I bought the suit) had used one that was too short.

The correct fix required cutting into the trilaminate material itself to gain the length needed to seat the zipper properly. This is not a reversible cut. I measured carefully, made the modification, and the zipper seated cleanly.

### 3. Seam Sealing

The suit's stitched seams were allowing water through — stitching creates needle holes, and over time the factory seam tape had broken down. I located the leaks by pressure-testing and watching for bubbles, then sealed every affected seam with contact cement followed by Aquatape.

### 4. Internal Repairs

## What I Learned

- Sourcing specialist materials (Seaskin, SC4000) takes research — there is no one shop that stocks everything
- Adhesive chemistry matters: mixing ratio, surface prep, and curing conditions all affect bond strength
- Fabricating your own tooling is often the only option — drysuit-specific jigs don't exist off the shelf
- Fume safety is real — a respirator is not optional with this class of adhesive
- Patience over speed: rushing adhesive cure time causes failures
